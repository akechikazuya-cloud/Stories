Listen, my relatives, come sit by the fire a while and let me tell you something my grandfather told me when I was young. I still remember that evening. The sky was turning red behind the hills, and the wind moved through the grass like it was carrying old voices. My grandfather looked at me and said, “You think the world speaks only through people, but that is because you have not learned to listen.” At the time I did not understand what he meant. I thought listening meant hearing words. Years later, when I was old enough to travel alone, I finally learned what he was trying to teach me. One autumn morning I left camp before sunrise and followed a narrow trail through the forest. I was proud of myself back then. I believed I knew many things. I thought strength was enough to solve every problem. The trees stood tall around me, and I walked without paying attention to anything except my destination. Then I noticed a raven flying above me. It landed on a branch ahead and called out loudly. I waved it away and kept walking. A little farther on, the raven appeared again and cried out once more. I laughed and said, “I have no time for bird talk.” I was young and foolish.

As the sun climbed higher, clouds gathered over the mountains. The air became heavy, and the forest grew strangely quiet. Soon I reached a river that I had crossed many times before. Usually it was calm, but that day the water moved fast and dark. I looked for the usual crossing place, but the river had changed. The current seemed stronger than I remembered. I told myself I was brave and stepped into the water anyway. Before long the river pushed against my legs with such force that I nearly fell. I struggled to keep my balance. Then I heard the cry of a raven. Looking up, I saw the same bird circling above me. For a moment I remembered my grandfather’s words. Instead of moving forward, I stepped back to the shore. Just minutes later a surge of water rushed downstream from a storm in the mountains. If I had continued crossing, the river would have carried me away. Standing there, soaked and shaking, I realized that maybe the raven had been trying to warn me all along.

I spent the rest of the day looking for another path. The storm arrived before I found one. Rain fell so hard that the world disappeared behind a curtain of water. I found shelter beneath a rocky overhang and waited. While I sat there, hungry and cold, I watched the rain strike the earth. The drops hit leaves, stones, and soil, yet each place answered differently. The leaves trembled, the stones glistened, and the earth drank deeply. I began to think about how everything responds according to its nature. The rain was the same, but the world received it in different ways. That is when I started asking myself whether people were any different. Some hear wisdom and ignore it. Some hear it and remember it. Some hear it and allow it to change them. I wondered which kind of person I had been.

As evening approached, the rain stopped. Mist rose from the ground, and the forest smelled fresh and alive. I continued walking until I came to a meadow where an old cedar tree stood alone. It was enormous, far older than any person I had ever known. I sat beneath it and rested. Looking at its twisted branches, I thought about all the storms it must have survived. Wind had bent it. Lightning may have scarred it. Winters had covered it with snow. Yet it remained standing. It was not the tallest tree in the forest, nor the straightest. But it endured. I remember touching its bark and thinking that strength was not about never bending. Real strength was knowing how to bend without breaking. That lesson stayed with me longer than any lesson learned from pride.

Night came, and the stars appeared one by one. Sitting there alone, I felt small beneath the vast sky. Yet I did not feel unimportant. Instead, I felt connected. The same stars had watched my ancestors. The same wind had crossed these lands long before I was born. The same earth supported every creature, from the smallest insect to the largest animal. In that moment I understood something simple but powerful: none of us walk alone. We depend on one another more than we realize. The hunter depends on the land. The land depends on balance. Families depend on trust. Communities depend on kindness. When one part suffers, all parts feel the effect, even if they do not notice it immediately.

The next morning I returned home. My grandfather was sitting outside, watching the sunrise. Before I could say anything, he smiled and asked, “Did the world speak to you?” I remember laughing because I finally understood his question. I told him about the raven, the river, the storm, and the cedar tree. He listened quietly. When I finished, he nodded and said, “Good. The world is always teaching. Most people are simply too busy talking to hear the lessons.” Those words have stayed with me throughout my life. Whenever I become too certain of myself, I remember the river. Whenever hardship comes, I remember the cedar tree. Whenever I think I know everything, I remember the raven. And whenever I sit beneath the stars, I remember that I am only one small part of a much larger story.

So that is what I wanted to share with you tonight, my friends. If you leave here remembering only one thing, let it be this: listen carefully. Listen to the people who came before you. Listen to the land beneath your feet. Listen to the quiet moments that most people rush past. Wisdom does not always arrive as a loud voice. Sometimes it comes as a bird’s cry, the movement of water, the sound of rain, or the silence beneath an old tree. If you learn to listen, truly listen, you may discover that the world has been speaking to you all along.
