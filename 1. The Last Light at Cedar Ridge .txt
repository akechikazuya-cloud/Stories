“You all ever notice how the forest seems quiet right before it wants to teach you something? I was thinking about that the other day, and it reminded me of a time when I was younger, when I believed I knew more than the trees, more than the river, maybe even more than my own grandfather. Back then, I walked fast and spoke fast. I thought wisdom was something you could collect the way you collect arrows or tools. My grandfather would laugh when he saw me rushing around. He would say, ‘The earth has never hurried a single sunrise, and yet every sunrise arrives.’ I heard those words many times, but I never really listened to them. Not until the day I got lost.”

“I remember waking before dawn and deciding I would go deeper into the hills than anyone else had gone with me before. I wanted to prove I was brave. The air was cold, and the grass held drops of water that shined like little stars. I carried food, a blanket, and all the confidence a young person can carry. As I walked, I kept thinking about how everyone would react when I returned. I imagined people listening to my stories and admiring me. Funny thing is, when you spend too much time imagining what people will think of you, you stop paying attention to what is actually around you. I missed signs I should have seen. I crossed a stream I should have remembered. I followed animal tracks without noticing where they led. Before long, the hills looked unfamiliar, and the sun was lower than I expected.”

“At first, I wasn’t worried. I told myself I knew what I was doing. I said, ‘I’ll find my way back in a few minutes.’ Then a few minutes became an hour. The shadows stretched longer. The wind changed direction. I climbed a ridge expecting to see something familiar, but every valley looked the same. That was the moment fear finally sat beside me. You know the kind of fear I mean. Not the loud fear that makes you shout. The quiet fear that asks questions. ‘What if you are wrong? What if you cannot find your way home? What if night comes first?’ Those questions followed me like a second shadow.”

“So I kept walking, but the more I walked, the less certain I became. Eventually I reached a small clearing. In the middle stood an old tree. It was wider than any tree I had ever seen. Its bark was scarred from storms that had come and gone long before I was born. I was tired, angry, and embarrassed. I sat beneath it and began talking to myself. I complained about the hills. I complained about the trails. I even complained about my grandfather’s advice. Then I stopped because I realized how foolish I sounded. The tree had stood there through countless seasons, and there I was, upset because one day had become difficult.”

“As I sat there, I noticed something. The tree did not struggle to be a tree. The grass did not struggle to be grass. The birds were not worried about whether they looked wise. Everything around me simply did what it was meant to do. The forest wasn’t rushing. The river wasn’t rushing. Only I was rushing. For the first time that day, I became still. I listened. Really listened. I heard water somewhere in the distance. I heard birds calling from one side of the valley. I heard the wind moving through branches. Those sounds had been there the whole time. The problem was not that the forest had stopped speaking. The problem was that I had never stopped talking.”

“So I waited until my breathing slowed. Then I stood and followed the sound of water. I moved carefully this time. I watched where I stepped. I paid attention to the shape of the land. Before long I found a stream. My grandfather had taught me something about streams. He said, ‘Water remembers where it is going even when people forget where they are.’ I smiled when I remembered that. I followed the stream downhill. The journey was still long, but it no longer felt hopeless. Every bend in the water felt like a conversation. Every stone seemed to say, ‘Keep going. One step is enough.’”

“Night arrived before I reached home, so I made a small camp. I sat beside a fire and watched sparks rise into the darkness. Looking at those sparks, I started thinking about all the times I had wanted people to see me as strong. Yet strength had not helped me find my way. Pride had not helped me. What helped me was humility. What helped me was slowing down enough to learn. I remember laughing quietly to myself because I realized the hills had given me exactly what I needed, though not what I wanted. I wanted a story about being fearless. Instead, I was getting a lesson about being honest.”

“The next morning I continued following the stream. The sunlight felt warmer, and my mind felt lighter. Then, around midday, I saw something I recognized—a rock formation near the edge of our territory. I cannot describe the relief I felt. It was as though the land itself had reached out and placed a hand on my shoulder. Soon after that, I saw smoke rising from our village. Home had never looked so beautiful. Every sound seemed precious. Children laughing. Dogs barking. People talking. Things I barely noticed before suddenly felt like gifts.”

“When I finally returned, my grandfather was sitting outside. He looked at me for a long moment. He did not ask where I had been. He did not scold me. He simply smiled and said, ‘You walk differently now.’ That surprised me. Out of everything that had happened, that was what he noticed. I sat beside him and told him everything. I told him about getting lost, about the old tree, about the stream, about my fear. He listened without interrupting. When I finished, he nodded and said, ‘Good. The land has become one of your teachers.’”

“I think about that day often. Not because I got lost, but because of what being lost showed me. People spend a lot of their lives trying to avoid mistakes, trying to avoid uncertainty, trying to avoid feeling small. But sometimes those moments are the doorway to understanding. A person who never feels lost may never learn how to listen. A person who never struggles may never discover patience. The earth teaches these lessons quietly. It does not shout. It waits.”

“So whenever I see someone rushing through life the way I once rushed through those hills, I remember that old tree. I remember sitting beneath its branches, convinced the world had failed me, when really I had failed to notice the world. And I remind myself that wisdom is not something you capture and carry away. Wisdom is a relationship. It grows each time you listen. It grows each time you pay attention. It grows each time you admit you do not know everything.”

“That is why, when the forest becomes quiet, I no longer think nothing is happening. I think something important is about to be heard. And when life becomes difficult, I try not to ask, ‘Why is this happening to me?’ Instead, I ask, ‘What is this trying to teach me?’ Most of the time, the answer does not arrive immediately. It comes like the sunrise my grandfather spoke about—slowly, without hurry, exactly when it is ready. And every time I remember that lesson, I feel grateful for the day I walked into the hills believing I knew everything and returned home finally ready to learn.”
