“You know, there was a season in my life when I became obsessed with reaching the top of things. Not just hills or mountains, but everything. I wanted to be the best hunter, the fastest runner, the person with the most respect, the one whose name was mentioned whenever people gathered around a fire. At the time, that seemed like a good way to live. I thought success was a peak somewhere in the distance, and if I climbed high enough, I would finally find happiness waiting there. Looking back now, I smile at that younger version of myself because he spent so much time staring at the mountaintop that he barely noticed the trail beneath his feet.”

“I remember one particular day during late spring. The snow had melted from the higher elevations, and the rivers were full from the season's runoff. The sky was clear, and the mountains stood against the horizon like ancient guardians. I decided to climb one of the tallest peaks in the area. I had seen it many times from camp, and every time I looked at it, I felt challenged by it. I told myself that if I stood on its summit, I would somehow prove something—not to anyone else, but to myself. At least, that's what I claimed. The truth is, a part of me hoped others would admire the accomplishment.”

“So before sunrise, I gathered my supplies and started walking. At first, everything felt easy. The lower trails were familiar, and my energy was high. I moved quickly, eager to make progress. Every time I looked up and saw the peak, I felt a surge of determination. But as the hours passed, the climb became steeper. Rocks shifted beneath my feet. The air grew thinner. My legs became tired. Yet instead of appreciating how far I had come, I kept focusing on how far I still had to go.”

“By midday, I found myself growing frustrated. The summit seemed no closer than it had earlier. Have you ever noticed how distance can play tricks on the mind? A mountain often appears close from afar, but once you begin climbing, it reveals how much effort it truly demands. Life is often the same way. We see someone else's success and imagine it happened quickly, forgetting the countless steps hidden beneath the achievement.”

“As I continued upward, I passed through a meadow filled with wildflowers. The colors were beautiful—reds, yellows, blues, and whites scattered across the landscape like pieces of the sky had fallen to earth. But I barely glanced at them. I was too focused on the summit. A little later, I crossed a stream flowing over smooth stones. The water was so clear I could see every pebble resting on the bottom. Yet I hardly noticed it. I kept telling myself, ‘Later. I'll enjoy these things on the way down.’”

“Eventually I reached a section of the mountain where the trail became difficult to follow. The path twisted through rocky ledges and patches of loose gravel. My progress slowed. Each step required attention. I found myself growing irritated by every obstacle. I complained about the terrain. I complained about the heat. I even complained about my own exhaustion. It seemed as though the mountain had become my enemy.”

“Then something happened that changed my thinking. I slipped. Not a dramatic fall, just enough to lose my balance and land hard on the ground. My pride was injured more than my body. I sat there feeling frustrated and embarrassed, even though no one was around to witness it. As I caught my breath, I happened to look beside me and noticed a small plant growing from a crack in the rock.”

“It was an ordinary plant, easy to overlook. Yet there it was, thriving in a place where survival seemed impossible. No rich soil. No protection. Just a narrow crack in stone. I remember staring at it for a long time. That little plant wasn't worried about reaching the summit. It wasn't competing with the mountain. It wasn't measuring its progress against anything. It was simply growing where it had been planted.”

“The thought stayed with me as I resumed climbing. Slowly, I began paying more attention to the world around me. I noticed the sound of distant birds. I noticed how the wind changed as I gained elevation. I noticed the patterns of sunlight moving across the valley below. Things I had ignored all morning suddenly seemed important. The mountain had not changed. My attention had.”

“Several hours later, I finally reached the summit. For a moment, I felt triumphant. The view stretched in every direction. Forests, rivers, hills, and distant peaks spread across the horizon. It was beautiful beyond words. Yet after a few minutes, something unexpected happened. The excitement faded. Not completely, but enough for me to realize something. The happiness I expected to find at the top was smaller than I imagined. The summit was wonderful, but it wasn't magical. It didn't transform me. It didn't answer all my questions.”

“I sat there looking across the landscape and began thinking about the meadow of flowers, the clear stream, the plant growing from stone, and all the moments I had rushed past. The truth became impossible to ignore. The climb had contained far more life than the summit. Most of the experience had happened on the journey, yet I had spent much of that journey wishing I were somewhere else.”

“That realization followed me all the way home. I started noticing how often people do the same thing. We tell ourselves we'll be happy when we achieve something. When we earn more. When we gain recognition. When we reach a certain goal. And those goals can be meaningful, but if we're not careful, we spend our entire lives postponing our appreciation for the present moment.”

“I thought about my grandparents. Some of the happiest moments I remembered with them weren't tied to major accomplishments. They happened during ordinary days—sharing food, listening to stories, walking through the woods, sitting beside a fire. None of those moments would have impressed anyone from the outside. Yet they became treasures because we were fully present for them.”

“By the time I returned to camp, the mountain had taught me a lesson I hadn't expected. I had started the day believing that fulfillment lived at the top. I ended the day understanding that fulfillment lives in attention. A person can stand on the highest peak and still feel empty if they never learn to appreciate the journey. Another person can walk an ordinary path and feel rich because they notice the beauty surrounding them.”

“So now, whenever I find myself becoming too focused on some distant goal, I remember that climb. I remember the flowers I nearly ignored. I remember the stream I almost walked past. I remember that tiny plant growing from solid rock. And I remind myself that life is not a race toward a summit. Life is a trail. Some parts are steep. Some parts are easy. Some parts are breathtaking. Some parts are difficult. But every step contains something worth seeing if we're willing to slow down and look.”

“And that is why, when young people ask me how to find happiness, I rarely tell them to chase it. Happiness is a strange thing. The harder you chase it, the farther away it seems. Instead, I tell them to pay attention. Notice the people beside you. Notice the lessons hidden in ordinary days. Notice the gifts that exist right now. Because one day you'll look back and realize that what seemed like small moments were actually the most important parts of the journey all along.”
