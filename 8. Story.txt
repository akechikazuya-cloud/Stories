Come closer, my relatives, and warm yourselves by the fire. The night is long, and I want to share something that happened many winters ago. I have told this story to my children and now to you, because some lessons are too important to keep to yourself. When I was younger, I believed that a person's worth came from how much they could accomplish alone. I thought asking for help was a sign of weakness. If there was work to be done, I wanted to be the one to do it. If there was a challenge, I wanted to face it by myself. I carried that pride everywhere, like a heavy bundle on my back, though I did not realize how heavy it was at the time. One morning, just after the first snow had covered the hills, I set out to hunt. The air was cold enough to sting my face, and each breath became a cloud before me. I remember feeling strong and confident. The world seemed quiet, and I thought nothing could go wrong.

As I traveled deeper into the hills, I noticed tracks in the snow. They belonged to a deer, and they looked fresh. I followed them eagerly, convinced I would return home with a successful hunt. The tracks wound through trees, across small streams, and over ridges. Hours passed. The deer remained out of sight, but I kept following. I became so focused on the trail that I stopped paying attention to everything else. The sun moved across the sky, and the shadows grew longer. Eventually I realized something troubling. I no longer recognized the land around me. The familiar landmarks had disappeared. The tracks I had followed so carefully had led me far from where I intended to go. For the first time that day, I felt uncertainty. I looked around and saw only snow-covered hills and endless trees. The confidence I had carried that morning began to fade.

I told myself not to worry. I was certain I could find my way back. So I chose a direction and started walking. An hour later I found myself standing in a place that looked exactly like where I had started. Every hill seemed the same. Every tree looked familiar. The forest had become a maze. The cold grew sharper as evening approached. I could feel fear creeping into my thoughts, though I fought hard not to admit it. I remember saying aloud, "I do not need help. I can solve this myself." Looking back now, I think I was trying to convince myself more than anyone else. The truth was that I was lost.

As darkness settled over the land, I decided to make a small shelter and wait until morning. I gathered branches and built a fire. Sitting there alone, I listened to the crackling flames and the distant sound of wind moving through the trees. The fire gave warmth, but it could not quiet my thoughts. I began thinking about my family waiting at home. They would be wondering where I was. They might be worried. For the first time, I understood that my actions affected more than just myself. I had always thought of independence as a great strength, but now I saw another side of it. If I insisted on carrying every burden alone, I was also denying others the chance to help me. I was acting as though I stood apart from the people who cared about me. That realization troubled me more than being lost.

The next morning, I woke before sunrise. The fire had burned low, and frost covered my blanket. As I stepped out into the pale light, I noticed something unusual. Near my shelter were the tracks of many small animals. Rabbits, foxes, and birds had all passed through during the night. Some tracks crossed each other. Some traveled together for a while before separating. Looking at them, I began to think about how every creature depends on others in some way. Even the strongest animals are part of a larger pattern. None of them survive entirely alone. The forest itself is built on relationships. Trees share the soil. Rivers feed the plants. Animals spread seeds. Everything gives and receives. Yet here I was, believing I needed no one.

I continued walking, moving more carefully than before. Around midday I heard a distant sound. At first I thought it was the wind, but then I recognized it. It was a human voice. I stopped and listened. There it was again. Someone was calling. My heart lifted. Soon I saw two people from my village making their way through the snow. They had come looking for me. When they reached me, they smiled and greeted me warmly. Neither one scolded me. Neither one mocked me for getting lost. They simply said, "Come, let us go home." I cannot fully describe the feeling that washed over me in that moment. Relief, gratitude, and humility all arrived together. The people I cared about had not abandoned me. They had shared my burden without being asked.

As we walked back, one of them pointed to a flock of geese flying overhead. They moved together in a great V-shape across the sky. He asked me why I thought they traveled that way. I told him I did not know. He explained that each bird takes a turn leading. When one grows tired, another moves forward. The flock reaches its destination because they share the work. I looked up at those birds and felt as though the world was speaking directly to me. The lesson was so simple that I wondered how I had missed it for so many years. Strength is not only found in standing alone. Sometimes true strength is found in accepting support and offering it in return.

When we finally arrived home, my family welcomed me with smiles and warm food. Sitting beside the fire that evening, I reflected on everything that had happened. I realized that I had spent much of my life trying to prove how capable I was. But life is not a contest to see who can carry the heaviest load. It is a journey meant to be shared. We all stumble. We all lose our way at times. There is no shame in that. The shame comes only when pride prevents us from reaching out or from accepting the hands extended toward us.

So, my relatives, whenever I remember that winter, I do not think about the deer I never found. I do not think about the cold or the fear. I think about the voices calling through the forest. I think about the geese crossing the sky. I think about the people who came looking for me when I could not find my own path. And that is why I tell you this tonight. If you ever find yourself carrying a burden that feels too heavy, remember that you do not have to carry it alone. We are connected like the trees in a forest and the birds in a flock. When we help one another, we become stronger than any one person could ever be. That is what the land taught me, and it is a lesson I hope you will carry with you long after this fire has faded into glowing embers.
